Multi-Agent Systems

About This Section

This section explores systems with multiple agents working together - coordinating, communicating, delegating, and collaborating to accomplish goals beyond what single agents can achieve. Multi-agent systems enable specialization, parallelism, robustness, and emergent capabilities. Understanding multi-agent patterns is essential for building sophisticated systems that can tackle complex, multi-faceted problems.

Contents

Multi-Agent Fundamentals

Why use multiple agents? Benefits of specialization, parallelism, and modularity. Covers when multi-agent systems are appropriate vs. single-agent systems, and the coordination overhead they introduce.

Agent Roles and Specialization

Designing specialized agents with specific capabilities: role-based decomposition, expert agents, generalist vs. specialist trade-offs, and how to divide responsibilities across agents.

Communication Patterns

How agents communicate: message passing, shared state, blackboards, and communication protocols. Covers synchronous vs. asynchronous communication, message formats, and ensuring message understanding.

Coordination and Task Allocation

Coordinating work across multiple agents: task allocation strategies, synchronization, conflict resolution, and ensuring agents work together effectively without stepping on each other.

Delegation and Hierarchies

Hierarchical multi-agent systems: manager-worker patterns, task delegation, supervisory agents, and organizing agents in hierarchies. Covers when hierarchical vs. peer-to-peer structures work best.

Collaboration Patterns

Cooperative agents working toward shared goals: joint problem-solving, distributed planning, shared memory, and patterns for agents that collaborate rather than compete.

Debate and Consensus

Multi-agent deliberation, voting, consensus building, and using multiple agents to improve decision quality through diverse perspectives and debate.

Swarm Intelligence

Emergent behavior from simple agents, decentralized coordination, and self-organizing systems. Covers when swarm patterns are appropriate and how global behavior emerges from local interactions.
